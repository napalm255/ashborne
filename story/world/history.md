# The Ashborne: Historical Context
## The Battle of Monocacy & The Ironveil

---

## The Battle of Monocacy — July 9, 1864

**Official history:** A daylong engagement between Union Major General Lew Wallace's Army of the Potomac (6,000 troops) and Confederate General Jubal Early's Second Corps (14,000 troops), fought along the Monocacy River southeast of Frederick. Union forces held the river crossing long enough to delay Confederate advance toward Washington, DC. Nearly 2,000 combined casualties over twelve hours. Strategic victory: Union. Tactical cost: high.

**What the official reports don't explain:** What soldiers reported seeing in the confusion and smoke. What one regimental surgeon documented in a letter to his wife that was never made public. What prompted three Union officers and two Confederate soldiers to desert their units simultaneously at precisely 3:47 PM on that July afternoon.

---

## The Witnesses: What They Saw

The fragmented accounts recovered by the Ironveil describe the same phenomenon from multiple perspectives:

- **Cold that moved independently** across the battlefield—not the ambient temperature, but localized zones where frost formed in July, where water froze while soldiers watched.
- **A presence** rather than a shape—something that could not be lined up and shot at because it had no clean borders, no substance to pierce.
- **Influence on the dying** — soldiers who had fatal wounds but did not die, who spoke in languages they did not know, who had to be physically separated from other wounded because their presence caused electrical equipment to fail and nearby men to experience overwhelming dread.
- **Feeding.** The thing was not attacking. It was *consuming*—not flesh, but something less tangible. Fear. Life force. The will to continue. Soldiers near the phenomenon reported that the will to fight simply evaporated.

By 3:47 PM, twelve soldiers and one civilian (a boy, approximately fourteen, who had been watching from a ridge) abandoned their positions. They walked toward the phenomenon instead of away. Three officers ordered pursuit. The pursuit was called off when the pursuing soldiers reported that the twelve soldiers simply *disappeared* into a zone of fog and cold, and when the fog cleared, they were gone.

One officer recorded in his journal: *"I cannot write what I saw. I know what I saw. I also know that if I write it, I will be dismissed as a coward or a lunatic. I choose instead to live with silence and the specific knowledge that something in Frederick County was never meant to be disturbed."*

## The Ironveil: Formation & Purpose

After the battle, five survivors of the anomaly—three Union soldiers, one Confederate soldier, and one civilian (Clara Marsh, a telegraph operator operating under the male pseudonym "C. Marsh")—made contact with each other. None of them intended to. All of them felt drawn toward Vivienne's family property in Frederick, where they met, exhausted and traumatized, on July 14, 1864.

They were joined by two clergymen: **Reverend Samuel Weiss** (Libertytown) and **Reverend Heinrich Tillman** (Middletown). Both had parishes whose congregations included people who had experienced the phenomenon. Both had ancestors from regions in Germany known for documenting occult presence and containment.

Seven people, frightened and traumatized, but also intelligent and methodical.

**The Ironveil** was not officially named until years later. In the early days, Clara Marsh referred to the group in her journal as *"the Sutured Order"*—the order that would stitch the wound in reality the Ashborne had created.

Their purpose was simple: the thing that had fed on the battlefield was still in Frederick. It was dormant, but not dead. It had been drawn to the bloodshed of Monocacy and had anchored itself to the location. The Five (later Seven) would spend the rest of their lives ensuring it never woke up. They would maintain the seal. They would protect Frederick from what it had found.

---

## The Sealing: Materials & Method

The Ironveil's founders were educated people. Two were military officers with advanced training. Clara Marsh understood electrical signals and resonance. The two clerics had access to historical texts on ritual containment that had been preserved in their families for generations.

They synthesized:
- **Period materials:** Iron and copper, sourced from local foundries, worked according to geometric specifications that predated the modern cipher
- **Geometric precision:** Not a ritual in the occult sense (though that's how it appeared), but a carefully calculated arrangement that created a standing wave pattern in the electromagnetic field itself—a harmonic cage built from period materials
- **A cipher:** A symbolic language that encoded the sealing parameters, the frequency, the geometry, and the prohibition against re-entry. This cipher was based on Vigenère principles but extended to incorporate esoteric symbols that represented not letters but *phases*—stages of the sealing itself.
- **Three sealing points:** The Best Farm (public site, disguised as cellar storage), the Libertytown Farmstead (hidden from public record), and a third location beneath Frederick's historic district (revealed only in Act II)

The sealing ritual was performed in stages across three weeks in July and August 1864. It required all seven members to be present for the final actualization. The cipher was written on the walls of the Libertytown cellar and burned into the stone of the Best Farm's hidden cellar. The geometric arrangements were set in place. The period materials were positioned according to specification.

On August 5, 1864, at sunset, the Seven completed the seal. The phenomenon ceased. The cold zone dissipated. The thing in Frederick went dormant.

One of the officers, **Colonel Marcus Harmon**, wrote in his personal journal: *"It is done. I do not know how it is done, nor do I fully comprehend what it was that we contained. I know only that it is sealed, that the seal is designed to hold for generations, and that someone will have to maintain it. I am establishing a covenant that my descendants will guard this knowledge and this seal. Whatever cost."*

His great-great-granddaughter is Edna Harmon, and she knows.

## The Maintenance: 1864–2025

For 161 years, Ironveil descendants maintained the seal through:

- **Periodic maintenance visits** to each sealing point, checking the materials, reinforcing the geometric configurations
- **Passing knowledge** through family letters, journals, and in-person instruction. Every generation, at least one member of each family line knew the full truth.
- **Defending the sealing points** from accidental disturbance—discouraging excavation, maintaining the Best Farm as a public exhibit so the cellar remained undisturbed, ensuring the Libertytown farmstead stayed abandoned
- **Watching for signs of weakening** — and there were signs. In 1923, one Ironveil member noted in a personal journal that the cold zone was expanding slightly. In 1962, another documented unusual wildlife behavior at the Monocacy site. In 1987, a Board directive was issued to keep a specific crate from the Monocacy excavation separate from the main Historical Society collection.

By 2000, the Ironveil was smaller. Families had scattered. Not all descendants chose to carry forward the knowledge. The seal was holding, but its guardians were aging.

By 2024, the Ironveil was fractured. Most members didn't know each other. The knowledge was preserved but passive. No one expected to *need* the seal. No one expected it to be threatened.

They didn't account for someone deliberately trying to *break* it.

---

## The Breach: Present Day (October 2025)

**The Keeper** has spent fifteen years studying the Ironveil's work. Not to maintain it, but to understand how to dismantle it. Whether driven by genuine belief that the Ashborne can be controlled, or by something darker, is revealed in Act II.

The Keeper has been:
- Acquiring Ironveil artifacts (the sealing apparatus components, ritual items, period materials)
- Mapping the sealing points with surgical precision
- Locating Ironveil descendants (some willingly, some not)
- Constructing a counter-apparatus designed to *invert* the seal rather than maintain it

By October 2025, the seal is cracking—not from natural decay, but from deliberate interference. The Keeper has breached one of the sealing points. The others are weakening. The phenomenon is leaking through.

Maddox Grey, a man who just wanted to find a budget vulnerability in a city IT network, finds instead the bleeding edge of a 161-year-old wound being deliberately opened.

## Clara Marsh: The Anomaly

One final note on the Ironveil's founder who doesn't fit the pattern:

Clara Marsh was a telegraph operator in Frederick during the war, operating under the pseudonym "C. Marsh" in official records. She was the only woman among the Seven. She had no family line to inherit knowledge from. She chose to stay in Frederick, never married, and spent sixty years documenting everything the Ironveil learned.

Her personal journals, discovered by Vivienne Colt in an estate sale in 1992, are the most comprehensive historical record of the sealing. They are also deeply personal—her fear, her commitment, her observations about how knowledge is carried by people rather than places.

One entry, dated 1901, reads:

*"The Seal holds. We have reinforced it three times in my lifetime. I have trained one successor from each family line to carry this knowledge after I am gone. Whether they will choose to maintain what we have begun, I cannot know. I can only ensure that if they do wish to know, the path is clear. Knowledge that is not written down is lost when the person dies. But knowledge that is written down waits. It is patient. It is true. That must be enough."*

The Ironveil continues because Clara Marsh wrote everything down.

# Character Profile — Wren Calloway

---

## Basic Info

| | |
|---|---|
| **Full Name** | Wren Rebecca Calloway |
| **Age** | 34 |
| **Occupation** | Cultural Resources Manager, Monocacy National Battlefield (National Park Service) |
| **Location** | Frederick, Maryland — rents a row house on East 3rd Street |
| **Companions** | Scout (Belgian Malinois mix, 4) and Finn (Golden Retriever, 7) |

---

## Appearance

Mid-height, lean from years of fieldwork. Her style is functional—hiking boots, work cargo pants with reinforced pockets, layers that shed or add depending on season. Her dark hair is kept in a braid or short, never down—too much work, too many occasions where a stray strand creates problems. Her eyes are a light brown, alert, with the particular attentiveness of someone trained to notice what others walk past.

She has a small scar on her left temple from a hiking accident at age twenty-two; it's faint enough that most people don't notice it until she points it out during one of her occasional self-deprecating stories. She carries herself with the quiet confidence of someone who knows her expertise is genuine and doesn't need to announce it.

Weathered hands. Calluses from digging and climbing. Her nails are kept short. She wears a worn silver ring on her right hand—family piece, Scandinavian pattern, nothing she talks about.

---

## Personality

**Methodical and precise.** Wren has spent nineteen years doing battlefield archaeology and cultural resources management. Every object matters. Every detail is recorded. She is comfortable with uncertainty in interpretation, but she is meticulous about evidence. She does not make leaps without foundation.

**Quietly funny.** Her humor is dry, occasional, and lands as a surprise because she doesn't telegraph it. She finds absurdity in bureaucracy, in human nature, in the specific situations that arise when you're trying to preserve history while everything around you wants to develop it into condominiums.

**Determined.** Once she commits to something, she follows through. This is both a strength and a limitation—she can be stubborn when she believes she's right, and she can be slow to change her mind when evidence contradicts her framework.

**Patient with systems, impatient with dismissiveness.** She will spend hours working through bureaucratic processes to get permits and access. She will not tolerate people who dismiss evidence because it's inconvenient or because it challenges their worldview.

**Observant to a fault.** She notices everything. Environmental details, behavioral patterns, inconsistencies. This is useful for fieldwork. It's sometimes exhausting in social situations, because she's aware of what people aren't saying as clearly as what they are.

**Protective of Finn and Scout.** She is generally reserved about her personal life, but her dogs are non-negotiable. They are working animals, trained animals, and she maintains their well-being with the seriousness most people reserve for human relationships.

---

## Background & Expertise

**Education:** Bachelor's degree in Archaeology from the University of Maryland, Master's in Historical Preservation (George Mason University). Dissertation focused on Civil War-era fortifications and how they've weathered 160 years of environmental stress.

**Experience:** Nineteen years in the National Park Service, primarily managing cultural resources at battlefield sites across Maryland and Virginia. She has cataloged artifacts, supervised excavations, written preservation reports, negotiated with contractors, and trained volunteers.

**Special expertise:**
- 19th-century American military history (particularly Civil War, 1860–1865)
- Battlefield archaeology and site analysis
- Artifact analysis and provenance research
- Oral history documentation
- Grant writing and project management
- Field research methodology

**Relevant knowledge:** Wren has been stationed at Monocacy for three years. She knows the battlefield intimately—every structure, every documented site, every historical photograph. She has access to archives, permission to conduct surveys, and clearance to explore areas the public doesn't visit.

---

## Current Situation (Act I, October 2025)

**Three months of anomalies.** Wren has been documenting unusual phenomena at the Monocacy site since late July:
- Cold spots appearing in open field with no meteorological explanation
- Equipment malfunctions (GPS units losing signal, cameras corrupting data) in specific locations
- Unusual animal behavior (birds abandoning nesting sites, deer avoiding specific areas)
- Staff and visitor reports of "feeling wrong" in certain locations

All of these observations are meticulously recorded in her field notebook. She has filed preliminary reports with her supervisor, who has suggested that the data is likely equipment failure and weather variation. Wren knows her own equipment is functioning correctly. She also knows her supervisor's response is standard bureaucratic dismissal.

**No framework for the data.** Wren can measure and document the anomalies, but she has no historical precedent for what she's observing. The Battle of Monocacy has been thoroughly researched. There's no mention of anything like this in any primary source. She's missing a framework to interpret what she's seeing.

**Meeting Maddox:** When she encounters Maddox at Monocacy on the evening of October 14 with a HackRF analyzer and a pitbull, her initial response is professional irritation (trespassing, restricted area, liability issues). Her second response is recognition—his equipment is detecting something *quantifiable* about the phenomenon she's been observing. When Scout responds to the same location as Dexter, in the same way, independently, Wren's framework shifts. This isn't equipment failure. This is something real.

## Role in Story

### Act I

**Initial arc:** Obstacle → Parallel investigator → Ally

She starts as a barrier to Maddox (he's trespassing, she can report him, she's NPS authority). She becomes a source of independent verification of the phenomenon. She becomes essential when cross-referencing his RF data with her historical research reveals the connection to Monocacy 1864.

**Key contributions:**
- Historical context: She knows the Monocacy battle intimately, can identify historical documents Maddox encounters, can interpret references to the Ironveil
- Independent data: Her three months of field observations create a timeline that predates Maddox's involvement
- Access: She has legitimate access to Monocacy, to park archives, to areas Maddox cannot reach alone
- Credibility: When they meet with Margaret Howell at the Historical Society, Wren's professional credentials open doors

**Relationship arc with Maddox:** Starts guarded. Wren is professional, initially skeptical of Maddox's methodology (he's operating outside proper channels, no institutional affiliation). Maddox sees her as a bureaucrat who will slow him down. By the end of Act I, they've learned to value what the other brings—Wren's historical rigor complements Maddox's technical agility. They're not in love yet, but they're functioning as a coherent investigative team.

### Acts II & III

**Expanding knowledge:** Wren becomes the interpreter of Ironveil documents, the expert on period context, the person who can read an 1864 journal entry and explain what it actually means. Without her, Maddox can detect the phenomenon but can't understand it.

**Personal cost:** As the investigation deepens, Wren's carefully maintained professional credibility becomes complicated. She's using park resources for non-official purposes. She's disappearing from work for investigation. She has to choose between her career and the investigation. She chooses the investigation.

**Relationship deepening:** The forced intimacy of dangerous investigation, combined with genuine intellectual respect, creates space for romantic development. It's not the focus of the story, but it's real. By Act III, they're together in a way that's irreversible.

**Final act:** In Act III, Wren's expertise becomes critical for the final confrontation. She knows the tunnel system better than anyone else. She can navigate Ironveil symbolism. She becomes not just co-investigator but co-solver.

## Scout (Belgian Malinois Mix)

**Age:** 4 | **Weight:** 55 lbs | **Training:** Search & rescue, NPS wildlife detection protocol, protection trained (though Wren rarely activates this)

Scout is all work-drive energy. She was originally trained through an NPS wildlife program for search operations—finding missing persons, locating specific scents, surveying terrain. She is fast, focused, and has the kind of intelligence that makes you realize dogs are making constant assessments about their environment.

She wears a utilitarian harness designed for fieldwork, and she knows the difference between "work mode" (harness on, full attention) and "off-duty" (harness off, still alert but less intense).

**In the story:** Scout's reactions to the anomalies at Monocacy mirror Dexter's without any shared training between them. She will not enter certain zones. She alerts on the same cold spots Dexter does. This independent confirmation is what makes Wren and Maddox take each other seriously.

Scout is loyal to Wren with a completeness that's almost unsettling if you think too hard about it. She will put herself between Wren and danger without hesitation.

---

## Finn (Golden Retriever)

**Age:** 7 | **Weight:** 72 lbs | **Training:** Companion, professional therapy temperament, has lived at three different battlefield sites

Finn is Scout's counterpoint. He is golden-colored, gentle-eyed, and radiates the kind of accessible friendliness that makes strangers want to stop and talk to him. He is also extremely intelligent, just in a different direction than Scout—Scout is kinetically smart, Finn is observationally smart.

Finn has lived at NPS sites his entire life, primarily with Wren. He is completely unbothered by history, monuments, or the weight of what battlefields represent. He knows who Wren is, and that's sufficient. He will nap in the sun anywhere.

**In the story:** Finn is why people talk to Wren. They approach to pet the dog, and end up discussing the battlefield. Finn is also why Maddox starts to trust the situation—a dog that anxious-to-please, that friendly, that observant, doesn't maintain that disposition if his handler is in genuine danger. If Finn is calm, it's a signal worth heeding.

In high-phenomenon areas, Finn stays calm when Scout is tense. This is both comforting and unsettling. It suggests that Finn perceives the Ashborne's presence differently than Scout does—not as a threat, but as an oddity worth noting and moving past.

## Friction with Maddox (Initial)

**Her perspective:** He's a trespasser with specialized equipment and zero understanding of proper methodology. He's operating outside institutional channels, which means his findings have no legal standing. He's treating this like a hacking problem when it's an archaeological problem. He talks to his dog like the dog is a person.

**His perspective:** She's a government bureaucrat who will file reports instead of acting. She'll prioritize process over urgency. She doesn't understand that the "proper channels" are too slow for something actively killing people. He respects her knowledge, but he doesn't trust her priorities.

**Reality:** They're both right and both wrong. Wren's methodical approach prevents leaps that would have sent Maddox down dead ends. Maddox's urgency keeps them moving when Wren's process would stall. By the end of Act I, they've learned to value this dynamic.

## Dialogue Notes & Voice

Wren speaks clearly and precisely. She uses technical terminology correctly, without apology. She doesn't explain her expertise; she expects people to either understand or ask questions. When she does explain something, she's patient but thorough.

She rarely uses contractions. "Do not" instead of "don't." "Cannot" instead of "can't." This isn't affected; it's just how she speaks. It makes her sound formal in casual situations, which is part of her personality.

She has a Maryland accent underneath everything—not strong, but audible in certain words (Monocacy, specifically, sounds different when she says it than when anyone else does).

Her humor, when it lands, is delivered deadpan. She doesn't pause for reaction; she just continues as if she said something serious.

## Relationships to Other Characters

**Maddox:** Initial friction, becomes investigative partnership and romantic partnership by Act III. Genuine intellectual respect combined with complementary skills.

**Dexter:** Wren likes Dexter. She respects that he's not a pet but a partner to Maddox. Scout and Dexter form a working respect—they communicate about the phenomenon in ways their handlers don't initially understand.

**Vivienne Colt:** Professional relationship from the beginning. Wren has visited Colt & Compass before (artifact sourcing, historical research). She and Vivienne share a respect for historical rigor. Vivienne becomes a resource for both Wren and Maddox.

**Edna Harmon:** When Wren and Maddox visit Walkersville, Wren takes the lead on building rapport with Edna. Wren's professional credentials and genuine interest in family history make Edna more open than she would be with Maddox alone.

**Nora Grey:** Acts as a bridge between Wren and Maddox's personal life. Nora approves of Wren immediately—she's capable, intelligent, doesn't coddle Maddox, and clearly has her own professional standing. By Act II, Nora and Wren are friendly outside the investigation context.

**Her supervisor (unnamed):** Professional tension. Her supervisor dismisses her field observations and is uncomfortable with how much time she's spending on "unofficial" investigation. This conflict comes to a head in Act II when Wren has to choose between her job and the investigation.
